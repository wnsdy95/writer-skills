# Methodology Map

## Purpose

Use this file to choose research methods for a book project. Combine methods based on evidence burden and reader promise. Do not over-research low-stakes creative questions, and do not under-research claims that can harm readers.

## Research Methods

| Method | Reveals | Best for | Strength | Failure mode | Output |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Literature review | existing knowledge and debates | scholarly, technical, health, policy, history | broad and citable | cherry-picking | annotated bibliography |
| Primary documents | what was recorded at the time | history, biography, true crime, policy | high evidentiary value | missing context | evidence map |
| Interviews | lived experience, expert judgment, memory | memoir, reportage, business, practical nonfiction | texture and specificity | memory distortion, bias | interview log |
| Surveys | patterns across many respondents | practical nonfiction, education, market insight | breadth | bad sampling | survey summary |
| Observation | actual behavior and setting | reportage, ethnography-like nonfiction, realism for fiction | concrete detail | observer bias | field notes |
| Field research | place, procedure, sensory and spatial facts | travel, food, history, fiction realism | embodied detail | anecdotal overreach | location dossier |
| Archive research | records, letters, newspapers, images | history, biography, true crime | depth and chronology | incomplete archive | archive log |
| Data analysis | measured patterns | business, policy, science, technical books | scale and precision | false causality | data notes |
| Case study | how one example works | business, education, practical books | narrative and application | non-generalizable | case brief |
| Competitor book analysis | market gap and reader expectation | all commercial books | positioning clarity | imitation | comp matrix |
| Fact-checking | claim accuracy | all nonfiction and realistic fiction | trust | narrow literal checks only | fact-check log |

## Method Selection

### Fiction

Use research to support plausibility, world texture, procedural accuracy, and emotional truth. Prioritize:
- reality research for settings, occupations, technologies, history, or subcultures
- sensitivity/context review when writing outside lived experience
- story bible for continuity
- expert sanity checks for procedures that matter to plot

Do not turn fiction research into exposition. Convert research into constraints, details, conflicts, and scene opportunities.

### Narrative Nonfiction

Prioritize:
- primary documents
- timeline reconstruction
- interviews with consent and notes
- archive research
- corroboration of contested claims
- legal/privacy review for named people

Separate:
- what the source proves
- what the author infers
- what is memory
- what is reconstructed for readability

### Practical Nonfiction

Prioritize:
- reader problem interviews
- case studies
- method testing
- competitor book analysis
- source quality checks for claims
- exercises/checklists

Avoid:
- promising universal results
- using anecdotes as proof
- disguising marketing copy as evidence

### Scholarly/Technical

Prioritize:
- literature review
- definitions and conceptual hierarchy
- technical validation
- worked examples
- citations
- peer or expert review

Track:
- source currency
- version-specific information
- assumptions
- edge cases

### Visual/Hybrid

Prioritize:
- visual references
- rights and asset notes
- page sequence research
- caption accuracy
- production constraints
- age/readability review for children's books
