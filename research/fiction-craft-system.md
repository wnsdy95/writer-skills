# Fiction Craft System

## Purpose

Use this system to design fiction that can sustain a full novel or series. The system prioritizes causality, character pressure, scene function, reader knowledge, setup/payoff, continuity, and revision order.

This is not a formula for one commercial genre. It is a control system for serious fiction work: every important choice must be traceable to character, conflict, premise, world rule, reader promise, or thematic pressure.

## Source-Grounded Principles

Use these sources as craft anchors, then translate them into project-specific rules.

- Purdue OWL Fiction Basics: plot is inseparable from character action; the story changes because characters respond to pressure. Use this to reject event chains where characters are only moved by author convenience.
- Purdue OWL fiction pitfalls: common early-draft failures include too much telling, weak scope control, shallow character development, and plot progression problems. Use this as a diagnostic checklist after each draft pass.
- Oregon State Guide to Literary Terms: foreshadowing plants suggestions or warnings about future events. Use this to distinguish real setup from decorative detail.
- The Center for Fiction on plot and pacing: a novel should cover a meaningful pressure window in a character's life; backstory enters only when it gives vital pressure or clues to the present story.
- Jane Friedman's story bible guidance and Arc Studio's bible/outline model: complex stories need a central repository for concept, setting, characters, storylines, locations, key beats, and continuity details.
- Fair-play mystery tradition, including Ronald Knox-style clue fairness: the reader should have access to the material evidence needed to reconstruct the solution, even if the interpretation is misdirected.

Source registry: see `source-bibliography.md`.

## Core Model

A novel is two stories at once:

- External plot: what happens, what pressure rises, what decisions change the situation.
- Internal arc: what the character believes, avoids, chooses, loses, and becomes.

Do not design these separately. A major plot event should pressure the character's inner flaw, and a character choice should alter the external plot.

Quality test:

| test | pass condition |
|---|---|
| character-action unity | The event happens because a character wants, fears, misunderstands, hides, or chooses something. |
| causal load | Each major beat creates a new condition that later scenes must obey. |
| pressure escalation | The cost of inaction, wrong action, or delayed truth increases. |
| moral/emotional specificity | The protagonist is not merely "challenged"; the story attacks a specific false belief, wound, desire, vow, or compromise. |
| reader contract | The book's opening promise is paid by the ending, even if the ending complicates that promise. |

## Premise Contract

Minimum premise fields:

- protagonist
- desire
- need
- obstacle/opposition
- stakes
- world pressure
- central dramatic question
- thematic pressure
- reader promise
- ending pressure: what must be transformed, revealed, lost, or chosen

Template:

```markdown
When [disruptive event] forces [protagonist] to [goal], they must confront [external obstacle] and [inner flaw], or [stakes].
```

Do not move to a chapter list until the premise can answer:

- Why this protagonist?
- Why now?
- Why can the protagonist not simply leave?
- What does the protagonist want that is not the same as what they need?
- What truth, relationship, institution, crime, curse, or wound will the ending force into the open?

## Character System

Track each major character by:

- desire: what they consciously want
- need: what would actually heal or transform them
- wound/false belief: why they resist the need
- secret: what changes the reader's or other characters' understanding
- mask: how they protect themselves socially
- pressure point: what makes them act
- choice pattern: what they choose under stress
- moral line: what they refuse to do until the plot pressures them
- contradiction: the trait that makes them harder than an archetype
- arc endpoint: changed, failed, corrupted, clarified, or sacrificed

Arc audit:

| arc stage | required evidence |
|---|---|
| initial strategy | How the character usually survives before the story breaks that strategy. |
| disruption | The event that makes the old strategy costly. |
| resistance | How the character protects the false belief. |
| pressured choice | A visible decision that worsens or clarifies the conflict. |
| reversal | The character sees the old strategy no longer works, or doubles down. |
| final choice | The ending externalizes the internal arc through action, sacrifice, confession, refusal, or transformation. |

## Plot Architecture

Every major beat must answer:

- What caused this?
- What changes because of it?
- Who makes a choice?
- What does the choice cost?
- What new question opens?
- What older setup is reinforced or paid off?
- What canon must be updated?

Avoid:

- coincidence solving central conflict
- new rule appearing at climax
- antagonist acting only because the plot needs it
- scenes that deliver information without pressure
- a twist that depends on withholding basic scene facts unfairly
- an ending that solves the surface problem while ignoring the book's opening promise

## Scene Design

Every scene needs:

- POV
- location/time
- scene goal
- opposition/conflict
- turning point
- new information
- emotional shift
- relationship shift
- setup planted or payoff triggered
- canon added
- knowledge change: who learns, mislearns, hides, or reveals what

A scene is weak if it can be removed without changing plot, character, relationship, information, atmosphere, theme, or reader expectation.

Scene function table:

| function | question |
|---|---|
| action | What physically changes? |
| decision | What choice can no longer be postponed? |
| discovery | What becomes known, and to whom? |
| concealment | What is hidden, misread, or lied about? |
| relationship | What bond shifts in trust, power, intimacy, debt, betrayal, or fear? |
| setup | What object, rule, phrase, clue, wound, or image gains future relevance? |
| payoff | What earlier promise is used, inverted, exposed, or fulfilled? |
| texture | What concrete detail makes the scene feel lived rather than summarized? |

## Setup and Payoff

Definitions:

- setup: a planted element that creates expectation, possibility, clue, rule, object, symbolic pattern, or emotional promise
- payoff: a later moment that uses the setup to create satisfaction, surprise, dread, revelation, or inevitability
- red herring: a setup that leads to a false but fair inference
- reinforcement: a later touch that keeps a setup alive without revealing it
- orphan setup: a planted element that no longer has a payoff
- unsupported payoff: a payoff that has not been earned by prior setup

Rules:

- A major payoff needs prior setup.
- A conspicuous setup needs payoff, conversion to red herring, or removal.
- A mystery clue must be fair in retrospect.
- A twist should feel surprising before the reveal and inevitable after it.
- Record every setup and payoff in `foreshadowing-payoff-ledger.md`.

## Fair-Play Mystery Matrix

For mystery, thriller, investigative fantasy, court intrigue, conspiracy, and twist-heavy fiction, every solution-dependent clue needs this matrix.

| field | use |
|---|---|
| clue_id | Stable ID connected to setup/payoff ledger. |
| true_meaning | What the clue actually proves. |
| visible_form | What the reader sees on the page. |
| misleading_inference | The plausible but false conclusion. |
| concealment_method | Misdirection, context gap, emotional distraction, unreliable interpretation, missing category, or social assumption. |
| reader_access | Whether the reader has enough information to notice the clue before the reveal. |
| detective_access | Whether the investigating character has the same, less, or more access than the reader. |
| solution_dependency | Required, supporting, thematic, or decorative. |
| fairness_test | After the reveal, can a careful reader point back to the clue without needing secret off-page information? |
| revision_action | Plant earlier, clarify, hide better, remove, or reclassify as red herring. |

Fairness rules:

- The final solution cannot depend on evidence never shown to the reader.
- Accidents, unexplained intuition, or sudden expert knowledge cannot solve the central mystery.
- Red herrings must be genuinely plausible from available evidence, not arbitrary noise.
- The story may hide meaning, motive, category, or context; it should not hide the existence of decisive evidence.

## Point of View and Knowledge

Separate:

- what the author knows
- what the reader knows
- what the POV character knows
- what other characters know
- what the character misunderstands
- what the narration is allowed to imply

Do not let a POV character narrate knowledge they do not have. Use `characters.md`, `timeline.md`, and `scene-ledger.md` to track knowledge changes.

POV audit:

- Is the scene filtered through one coherent consciousness or an intentional omniscient stance?
- Does the narration reveal facts the POV character cannot perceive?
- Is the reader's knowledge ahead of, behind, or equal to the protagonist's knowledge?
- Is suspense created by uncertainty, or by unfairly hiding basic facts?

## World Rules

World rules are not only for fantasy. Every novel has rules: legal, social, technological, institutional, familial, historical, magical, psychological, or genre rules.

Each rule needs:

- domain
- rule
- exception
- cost/limit
- first demonstration
- contradiction ban
- payoff potential

If a rule can solve any problem without cost, it will weaken plot tension.

## Series and Long-Form Continuity

Long projects need a story bible before drafting passes become expensive.

Track:

- canon facts and retcons
- names, terms, spellings, and banned variants
- character desire, wound, secret, relationship state, and knowledge state
- timeline in actual chronology and narrative disclosure order
- recurring locations and world rules
- open plot threads
- setup/payoff pairs
- style and voice constraints
- unresolved continuity issues
- revision queue

Do not rely on chat history for this memory. The manuscript and story bible are the source of truth.

## Revision Order

Revise from large to small:

1. central promise and ending
2. plot causality
3. character arcs
4. timeline and continuity
5. setup/payoff and fair-play clue access
6. scene function
7. POV and information flow
8. pacing
9. voice and dialogue
10. line polish
11. Korean style, spelling, spacing, and punctuation pass when writing Korean

Do not line-edit scenes that may be cut or structurally moved.

Revision gates:

| pass | stop condition |
|---|---|
| macro | Ending, central conflict, protagonist arc, and reader promise do not align. |
| causal | Major beats cannot explain why the next major beat becomes necessary. |
| continuity | Canon, timeline, clue state, or character knowledge contradicts itself. |
| scene | A scene lacks goal, conflict, turn, or consequence. |
| prose | The story works structurally, but voice, rhythm, clarity, and register need refinement. |
| proof | Remaining work is spelling, punctuation, typography, and production consistency. |
