# Book Type Playbooks

## Fiction

Classification signals:
- invented events, characters, or worlds are central
- reader promise depends on narrative satisfaction
- truth is emotional, thematic, or plausible rather than documentary

Core questions:
- What does the protagonist want?
- What do they need but resist?
- What pressure forces change?
- What question pulls the reader forward?
- What must be set up before the ending can feel earned?

Required research:
- genre conventions
- setting and procedure realism
- world rules
- sensory and cultural detail
- story bible for long or complex works

Structure pattern:
- premise
- character arc
- external plot
- scene sequence
- setup/payoff ledger
- ending requirements

Common failures:
- events happen without causality
- character arc is separate from plot
- world rules change for convenience
- clues appear only at payoff
- scenes only explain information

## Narrative Nonfiction

Classification signals:
- real people, events, memory, archives, or interviews are central
- narrative scenes are used to carry factual material

Core questions:
- What is known?
- How is it known?
- Who says so?
- What is remembered, inferred, reconstructed, or documented?
- What are the ethical risks?

Required research:
- timeline
- evidence map
- interviews
- archive/primary sources
- privacy and defamation review

Structure pattern:
- narrative spine
- chronological or thematic sequence
- source-backed scenes
- reflection/analysis layer
- risk register

Common failures:
- memory treated as documentary fact
- scene invention not disclosed or controlled
- real people flattened into plot devices
- allegations outpace evidence

## Practical Nonfiction

Classification signals:
- reader wants a method, change, solution, or framework
- book succeeds if reader can apply it

Core questions:
- What reader problem is solved?
- What transformation is promised?
- What steps are repeatable?
- What evidence supports the method?
- Where can the promise be overclaimed?

Required research:
- reader interviews
- case studies
- competitor analysis
- practical testing
- claim review

Structure pattern:
- problem
- promise
- framework
- step sequence
- examples
- exercises/checklists
- troubleshooting

Common failures:
- vague motivation instead of method
- universal promise from personal anecdote
- too many frameworks
- no practice path

## Scholarly/Technical

Classification signals:
- knowledge precision and conceptual structure matter
- readers expect definitions, evidence, examples, and citations

Core questions:
- What argument or system is being taught?
- What does the reader already know?
- What must be defined first?
- What examples prove understanding?
- What sources anchor claims?

Required research:
- literature review
- terminology map
- technical validation
- worked examples
- citation plan

Structure pattern:
- scope
- definitions
- concepts
- argument/system
- examples
- edge cases
- practice/application

Common failures:
- expert blind spots
- undefined terms
- examples too late
- citations detached from claims

## Visual/Hybrid

Classification signals:
- page, image, spread, panel, diagram, recipe, map, or caption is structural

Core questions:
- What does image do that text should not?
- What must be read in sequence?
- What is the page/spread unit?
- What rights or production constraints apply?
- How does the reader navigate?

Required research:
- visual references
- page plan
- image/caption source tracking
- production specs
- age/readability checks

Structure pattern:
- visual concept
- page/spread plan
- image list
- text/caption system
- production notes

Common failures:
- images decorative only
- text repeats image
- no rights trail
- spread pacing ignored

## High-Risk Specialist Books

Classification signals:
- claims can affect health, liberty, money, reputation, privacy, or safety

Core questions:
- What could readers do because of this book?
- What claims require expert review?
- Whose privacy or reputation is implicated?
- What must be softened, sourced, or removed?

Required research:
- high-quality sources
- expert review
- legal/ethical review
- sensitive claims log
- revision requirements

Common failures:
- advice presented as universal
- current standards ignored
- named people exposed unnecessarily
- source certainty overstated
